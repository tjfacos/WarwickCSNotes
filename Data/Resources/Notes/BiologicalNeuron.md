# Biological Neuron

Your body needs to **sense** things, think, and **respond** appropriately. To do this, the body relies on a **Central Nervous System (CNS)** which consists of two parts:
- the brain 
- the spinal cord 

The body also has a **Peripheral Nervous System (PNS)** which covers everything around the CNS. 

The fundamental building block of the CNS and PNS is the **neuron** (also called a *nerve cell* or *neural processing unit*), and there are about 100 billion ($10^11$) neurons in your brain and somewhere between 50 million ($5 \times 10^7$) and a billion ($10^9$) in the spinal cord. 

These neurons aren't all identical: there are sensory neurons, relay neurons (also called *interneurons*), and motor neurons. 

We'll go into depth on the types of neurons mentioned above, what neurons do, and the components of the neuron. 

## Types of Neuron

| Type | Location | Role |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Sensory Neuron | Inside sense organs (eye, ear, nose, tongue) | Receive stimuli from the outside world |
| Motor Neuron | Brain and spinal cord | Send signals from the brain to the muscles |
| Relay Neuron (Interneuron) | Brain and spinal cord | Acts in between sensory neurons and motor neurons |

## How a Neuron works

A neuron works by transmitting an electrical signal, called an **action potential**. 

Everything about a neuron is about receiving these action potentials, deciding whether the combination of received signals is strong enough to **activate** the neuron, and (if activated) transmitting an action potential to other neurons. 

## Structure of the Neuron 

There are 4 components of a neuron we discuss in this module: 
- **Dendrites:** These receive action potentials from other neurons
- **Soma:** This is the body of the neuron where action potentials are received and normal cell stuff happens: the nucleus is here, DNA is stored here, and proteins are made here
- **Axon:** This transmits an action potential to other neurons
- **Synapse:** The point at which two neurons meet

>![info]+ Axon Hillock
> The Axon Hillock is a 5th component which is mentioned (but not included as one of the 4 components for some reason).
> 
> It is in between the Soma and the Axon - it is where potentials are summed and where an action potential down the axon starts

### Dendrites

Dendrites receive signals from other neurons. 

To do this, they have **dendritic spines** on the surface of the dendrite which receives input from other neurons. Each spine corresponds to one synapse (connected to one other neuron).

Dendrites then transmit the action potential to the Soma. 

### Soma 

Body of the neuron where action potentials are received from the dendrites and normal cell stuff happens: has a nuceleus, DNA is stored, and proteins are made. 

The action potentials are transmitted to the Axon Hillock. 

### Axon Hillock

Action potentials are received from the Soma. 

If the strength of the action potentials exceeds the threshold limit of the Axon Hillock, then an action potential will be fired down the axon. 

### Axon

Carries the action potential from the Soma to the other neurons. 

Two main factors which determine the speed of the action potential:
- the diameter of the axon; the bigger the diameter, the faster the transmission
- whether it is covered with a **myelin sheath** i.e. if the axon is myelinated. If it is myelinated, the transmission is faster

>![info]- Myelin Sheath
> The **myelin sheath** is a fatty covering which act as an insulator for axons, so the action potential remains strong and doesn't dissipate/leak outwards.

The axon ends in a bunch of **axon terminals** which is where the synapses connecting the axon to the dendrites of other neurons are.
